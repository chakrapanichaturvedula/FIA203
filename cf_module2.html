A bond is a loan in tradeable form. The firm borrows money from investors, promises to pay a fixed coupon (interest) every period, and repay the face value (principal) at maturity. Bond price = present value of all future cash flows discounted at the market rate (YTM).

Anatomy of a bond

Face value (Par value)

The principal amount repaid at maturity. In India, typically ₹1,000 for corporate bonds, ₹100 for G-Secs. This is NOT the market price.

Coupon rate

Annual interest as a % of face value, fixed at issuance. A 7% coupon on ₹1,000 pays ₹70/year regardless of what happens to market rates.

Coupon payment

= Coupon rate × Face value. Usually paid semi-annually in the US; annually in India for G-Secs. Cash the bondholder actually receives each period.

Maturity date

The date when the face value is repaid. G-Secs in India range from 91-day T-bills to 40-year bonds. Corporate bonds typically 3–10 years.

Yield to maturity (YTM)

The total return earned if held to maturity. It is the discount rate that makes the PV of all future cash flows equal to the current price. YTM = cost of debt for the firm.

Current yield

= Annual coupon / Current price. Quick approximation of bond return. Ignores capital gain/loss at maturity — less accurate than YTM.

The price–yield relationship

The most important bond rule: When market interest rates rise, bond prices fall. When rates fall, prices rise. They move in opposite directions — always.

INDIAN MARKET EXAMPLES

G-Sec 7.26% 2033: A Government of India bond with 7.26% coupon, maturing in 2033. Traded on the RBI's NDS-OM platform. When RBI raises the repo rate, this bond's price falls.

HDFC Bank bond: HDFC Bank issued ₹5,000 cr of 5-year bonds at 7.80% in 2023. The coupon is fixed — but if you buy it in the secondary market when yields are 8.2%, you pay less than face value.

Reliance Industries NCD: Non-Convertible Debentures are corporate bonds in India. Reliance's bonds are rated AAA — lower risk means lower YTM than a BBB-rated firm's bond.

Unlike bonds, stocks have no maturity date and no promised cash flows. A stock's value = PV of all future dividends, discounted at the required return on equity (Re). This is the Dividend Discount Model (DDM). The same Re that CAPM produces in Module 4 is used here as the discount rate.

Types of equity valuation models

Zero-growth DDM

Dividends stay constant forever — like a preference share. P₀ = D/Re. This is simply a perpetuity formula.

Constant-growth DDM (Gordon Growth Model)

Dividends grow at a constant rate g forever. P₀ = D₁/(Re−g). Most widely used for stable, dividend-paying firms.

Price-Earnings (P/E) ratio

Market price divided by EPS. P/E = P₀/EPS. Used for quick relative valuation — compare a firm's P/E to its sector average or historical P/E.

Key assumptions and limits of DDM

g must be less than Re. If the growth rate equals or exceeds the required return, the denominator (Re−g) becomes zero or negative — the formula breaks down. In practice, g cannot exceed the long-run GDP growth rate forever.
Connection to Module 4 and 5: Re in the DDM is the same Re from CAPM. P₀ = D₁/(Re−g) and Re = Rf + β(Rm−Rf) are two sides of the same equation. A higher beta stock has a higher Re — and therefore a lower stock price for the same dividend stream.
INDIAN MARKET EXAMPLES

Hindustan Unilever (HUL): A classic constant-growth DDM stock. Stable FMCG business, consistent dividends growing ~5–7% per year. With Re ≈ 9%, P = D₁/(0.09−0.06) — a meaningful, calculable fair value.

Infosys: Paid ₹34 dividend in FY24. Strong payout policy. Suitable for DDM valuation. Market price vs DDM fair value signals over/undervaluation.

Zomato: Zero dividend, high growth phase. DDM doesn't apply. Analysts use Free Cash Flow models instead — the firm will return cash to shareholders only much later.

P/E in practice: Nifty 50 historically trades at 20–25x P/E. A stock trading at 35x P/E in a 22x market is "expensive" — investors expect higher future growth.

Zero-growth DDM (preference share / constant dividend)
P₀ = D / Re
P₀Current stock price (fair value today)
DConstant annual dividend (same every year, forever)
ReRequired return on equity (from CAPM or market data)
Example: Preference share pays ₹12 dividend forever, Re = 8% → P₀ = 12/0.08 = ₹150. Same as the perpetuity formula.
Constant-growth DDM — Gordon Growth Model
P₀ = D₁ / (Re − g)
P₀Fair value today
D₁Expected dividend next year = D₀ × (1+g)
ReRequired return on equity (must be > g)
gConstant dividend growth rate forever
HUL example: D₀ = ₹22, g = 5%, Re = 9% → D₁ = 22×1.05 = ₹23.1 → P₀ = 23.1/(0.09−0.05) = ₹577.50
Rearranging DDM — solving for Re (Cost of Equity)
Re = D₁/P₀ + g
D₁/P₀Dividend yield — income return
gCapital gain yield (stock price grows at same rate as dividends)
Module 5 link: This is the Dividend Growth Model for cost of equity. You use it in WACC as an alternative to CAPM. Same Re, two methods to estimate it.
Price-Earnings ratio
P/E = P₀ / EPS
P₀Current market price per share
EPSEarnings per share (trailing or forward)
Valuation use: Estimated Price = Industry P/E × EPS. If sector P/E is 22x and firm EPS is ₹50, estimated fair price = ₹1,100. Simple but ignores growth differences between firms.
