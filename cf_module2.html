Bond & Stock Valuation

CO2 · COMPUTE BOND AND STOCK PRICE

1. Bond Basics Ch. 8

A bond is a debt instrument where the issuer promises to pay periodic coupon interest and repay the face value at maturity. In India, Government Securities (G-Secs) and corporate bonds trade on the BSE Bond Platform and RBI's NDS-OM.

💵

Face Value (FV)

Par value paid at maturity. In India, G-Secs typically have FV = ₹100 per unit.

🎫

Coupon Rate

Annual interest rate on face value. A 7.26% G-Sec pays ₹7.26 per year on ₹100 FV.

📅

Maturity

When the FV is repaid. India's benchmark G-Sec is the 10-year bond.

2. Bond Pricing Ch. 8

A bond's price is the present value of all future cash flows — coupons plus face value — discounted at the market required rate (YTM).

Bond Price
P = C × [1–1/(1+y)t]/y  +  FV/(1+y)t
C = annual coupon  |  y = YTM (required yield)  |  t = years to maturity  |  FV = face value

Key Inverse Relationship: When market yields rise, bond prices fall — and vice versa. This is the most important rule in fixed income.

Indian G-Sec Example

A 7.26% G-Sec 2032 (10-year, FV = ₹100) with market YTM = 7.50%:

Annual coupon = ₹7.26. P = 7.26 × [1–1/(1.075)¹⁰]/0.075 + 100/(1.075)¹⁰

P = 7.26 × 6.864 + 100 × 0.485 = ₹49.83 + ₹48.52 = ₹98.35 (trading at a discount since YTM > coupon)

3. Yield to Maturity (YTM) & Current Yield Ch. 8

🎯

YTM

The single discount rate that makes PV of all cash flows equal to the current price. It's the bond's IRR — total return if held to maturity.

📊

Current Yield

Annual Coupon / Current Price. Simple but ignores capital gain/loss at maturity. Current Yield = C / P

💡

RBI Benchmark: The 10-year G-Sec yield is India's benchmark risk-free rate — used to price corporate bonds, home loans, and infrastructure financing. When RBI hikes repo rates, G-Sec yields rise and all bond prices fall.

4. Stock Valuation — Dividend Discount Model Ch. 9

A stock's intrinsic value equals the present value of all future dividends. The Dividend Discount Model (DDM) is the cornerstone of equity valuation.

Gordon Growth Model (Constant Growth DDM)
P₀ = D₁ / (r – g)
D₁ = next year's expected dividend  |  r = required return on equity  |  g = constant dividend growth rate
Infosys Valuation Example

Infosys paid a dividend of ₹34 last year (D₀). Dividends are expected to grow at 8% p.a. Required return = 13%.

D₁ = 34 × 1.08 = ₹36.72

P₀ = 36.72 / (0.13 – 0.08) = 36.72 / 0.05 = ₹734.40

5. P/E Multiple & FCFE Approach Ch. 9

Beyond DDM, analysts use Price/Earnings (P/E) multiples and Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) for valuation — especially when companies don't pay dividends.

📐

P/E Multiple

P = EPS × Industry P/E. If Nifty IT sector P/E = 28× and TCS EPS = ₹120, implied price = ₹3,360.

💸

FCFE Model

Value = FCFE₁ / (r–g). FCFE = Net Income + Depreciation – CapEx – ΔWorking Capital + Net Borrowing.
