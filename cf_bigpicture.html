THREE JUNCTIONS WHERE STUDENTS LOSE THE THREAD
01
CAPM → Cost of equity → WACC
Students learn CAPM in Module 4 as a "risk and return" topic — expected return, beta, the security market line. Then in Module 5 they see Re = Rf + β(Rm − Rf) again as a formula for the cost of equity. It feels like a new topic. It is not.

CAPM is the machine that produces Re. Once you have Re, it is one of only two inputs to WACC. Module 4 is not a standalone chapter — it is the factory that supplies the equity cost used in Module 5. Without this link, both modules feel disconnected.
Re = Rf + β(Rm − Rf) → plug Re into WACC → use WACC as discount rate
02
Bond valuation YTM = cost of debt
In Module 2 you learn to price a bond: discount the coupon stream and face value at the YTM. The YTM is the rate that makes the PV equal the market price.

In Module 5 "cost of debt" appears as if from nowhere. It is not new. The YTM is the cost of debt. The rate the bondholder earns is exactly the rate the firm pays. The same number, viewed from two sides. After-tax cost = YTM × (1 − Tax rate), because interest is tax-deductible in India.
Cost of debt Rd = YTM × (1 − Tc)
03
WACC as discount rate — three uses, one logic
Students see WACC used in three different places and think they are different formulas. They are the same logic applied at different levels:

Stock valuation (M2): Discount dividends at Re (cost of equity only — no debt).
Capital budgeting (M3): Discount project cash flows at WACC (blended cost of all capital).
Firm valuation: Discount firm-level free cash flows at WACC to get enterprise value.

In every case you are answering the same question: what is a future cash flow worth in today's money, given the risk and cost of the capital used to generate it?
PV = CF₁/(1+r) + CF₂/(1+r)² + … where r = WACC
04
The balance sheet is both the start and the end
The balance sheet is not just an accounting statement. It is a map of all three decisions:

Right side (Debt + Equity): These are the sources of capital. Their costs feed into WACC. Capital structure (how much debt vs equity) is studied in CF-II.
Left side (Assets): This is where capital is deployed. Every project accepted through capital budgeting shows up here as a fixed asset.

The loop closes: the firm raises capital (right side), computes the cost of that capital (WACC), uses WACC to evaluate investments (NPV), and the accepted projects become the assets (left side). Rejected projects save the capital for better uses.
Accepted NPV > 0 projects → fixed assets on the balance sheet
