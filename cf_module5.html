Every rupee of capital the firm uses has a cost. Equity holders demand a return (Re) for the risk they bear. Debt holders demand interest (Rd) for lending. Before computing WACC, we must estimate each component carefully. This tab ties together Modules 2 and 4 — YTM from bond valuation gives Rd; CAPM from risk & return gives Re.

Cost of equity — two methods

Method 1 — CAPM

Re = Rf + β(Rm − Rf). Uses market beta, G-Sec yield, and market risk premium. More objective and widely used. Requires a listed stock with reliable beta estimates.

Method 2 — Dividend Growth Model

Re = D₁/P₀ + g. Rearranged Gordon Growth Model. Simpler inputs, but requires stable dividends and a reliable estimate of g. Best for mature, dividend-paying firms.

Which method to use?

Use both and average the results where possible. CAPM is preferred for volatile or non-dividend firms. DGM suits stable dividend payers like HUL or ITC.

Cost of debt — and the tax shield

Pre-tax Rd = YTM on the firm's existing bonds — exactly what you computed in Module 2.

But interest payments are tax-deductible in India (Section 36 of Income Tax Act). This creates a "tax shield" — the government effectively subsidises part of the interest cost.

After-tax Rd = YTM × (1 − Tc)

Always use the after-tax cost of debt in WACC. Using the pre-tax rate overstates the true cost of debt and understates WACC.

The four-module chain

Module 1 (TVM) → discounting concept · Module 2 (Bond valuation) → YTM = pre-tax Rd · Module 4 (CAPM) → Re · Module 5 (WACC) → combines Re and Rd into the hurdle rate used in Module 3's NPV calculations.

Every formula in this module is a connection point, not a standalone concept.

WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital) blends the costs of equity and debt in proportion to their share of total financing. It is the firm's hurdle rate — the minimum return any project must earn to cover the cost of the capital used to fund it.

Why market value weights — not book value?

Always use market value weights in WACC, not book value weights.

Book value of equity = what shareholders originally invested (historical cost). Market value = what the equity is worth today (shares × current price). These can differ enormously — Infosys book value of equity is a fraction of its market cap of ₹6+ lakh crore.

WACC represents the current opportunity cost of capital. It must use current market values. Book values are backward-looking accounting figures irrelevant to today's financing decisions.

WACC as hurdle rate — accept/reject logic

Accept a project if NPV > 0 when discounted at WACC, or equivalently if IRR > WACC.

Every rupee the firm invests is funded by a blend of equity (costing Re) and debt (costing Rd after-tax). WACC is what that blend costs per year. A project earning less than WACC destroys value — the firm pays more for its capital than the project earns. A project earning more than WACC creates value — the surplus accrues to shareholders.

TATA MOTORS — FULL WACC CALCULATION

Capital structure (market values): Equity (market cap) = ₹2,00,000 cr · Debt = ₹80,000 cr · V = ₹2,80,000 cr

Weights: E/V = 200/280 = 71.4% · D/V = 80/280 = 28.6%

Cost of equity (CAPM): Rf=7.1%, β=1.30, MRP=5.5% → Re = 7.1 + 1.30×5.5 = 14.25%

Cost of debt (after-tax): Bond YTM=8.5%, Tc=25% → Rd = 8.5×0.75 = 6.375%

WACC = 0.714×14.25 + 0.286×6.375 = 10.17 + 1.82 = 11.99% ≈ 12%

Any Tata Motors project with IRR above 12% (or NPV > 0 at 12% discount rate) should be accepted.

WACC — core formula
WACC = (E/V) × Re + (D/V) × Rd × (1 − Tc)
EMarket value of equity (market cap = shares × price)
DMarket value of debt (use book value if bonds not actively traded)
VE + D = total market value of firm's capital
ReCost of equity from CAPM or DGM
Rd(1−Tc)After-tax cost of debt — YTM adjusted for tax shield
Tata Motors: WACC = (200/280)×14.25% + (80/280)×6.375% = 10.18% + 1.82% = 12.0%
NPV decision rule using WACC
NPV = −C₀ + Σ CFt / (1 + WACC)ᵗ
WACCThe discount rate for all project cash flows — reflects the firm's blended cost of capital
C₀Initial investment at time 0
CFtIncremental after-tax cash flows (from Module 3)
The complete chain: Module 3 gives the cash flows. Module 5 gives the discount rate (WACC). NPV = PV(cash flows) − Investment. If NPV > 0, accept.
Capital structure weights
E/V = E / (E + D)    D/V = D / (E + D)
E/VEquity weight — proportion of total capital that is equity
D/VDebt weight — proportion that is debt. E/V + D/V = 1.
Example: Market cap ₹300 cr, debt ₹200 cr → V = ₹500 cr. E/V = 300/500 = 60%. D/V = 200/500 = 40%. WACC = 0.60×Re + 0.40×Rd(1−Tc).
