Every investment involves risk — the chance that the actual return differs from what was expected. Investors demand a higher return for bearing higher risk. But not all risk is rewarded: only systematic (market) risk earns a premium. Firm-specific risk can be diversified away for free.

Measuring return and risk for a single asset

Expected return E(R)

The probability-weighted average of all possible returns. Σ pᵢ × Rᵢ. The "centre of gravity" of the return distribution.

Variance σ²

Average squared deviation from the mean. σ² = Σ pᵢ × (Rᵢ − E(R))². Measures spread of outcomes. Units are (%²) — not directly intuitive.

Standard deviation σ

Square root of variance. Same units as return (%). The most common risk measure. σ = 30% means returns typically deviate ±30% from the mean.

Covariance

Measures how two assets move together. Positive → move same direction. Negative → move opposite. Used to calculate portfolio risk.

Correlation (ρ)

Standardised covariance: ρ = Cov(A,B)/(σA × σB). Ranges −1 to +1. ρ = −1: maximum diversification. ρ = +1: no diversification benefit.

Systematic vs unsystematic risk

Total risk = Systematic risk + Unsystematic risk

Unsystematic risk (firm-specific, diversifiable): a factory fire, a CEO scandal, a product recall. Holding many stocks causes these events to average out — they cancel each other. The market does NOT pay a premium for this risk because rational investors diversify it away.

Systematic risk (market risk, non-diversifiable): a recession, an RBI rate hike, a global financial crisis. Affects ALL stocks simultaneously. No amount of diversification eliminates it. This is the only risk the market compensates you for bearing.

Diversification — the only free lunch in finance

When two assets are not perfectly correlated (ρ < 1), combining them reduces portfolio risk without reducing expected return. As you add more stocks, unsystematic risk falls. Beyond ~20–30 stocks, most firm-specific risk is eliminated and only systematic risk remains.

Key insight: Portfolio standard deviation is LESS than the weighted average of individual standard deviations (when ρ < 1). The lower the correlation, the greater the risk reduction. This is the mathematical foundation of diversification.
NIFTY 50 EXAMPLES

ITC + TCS: ITC (FMCG, stable demand) and TCS (IT services, USD revenues) have low mutual correlation. Combining them reduces portfolio volatility well below the average of both individually.

Reliance + ONGC: Both energy sector, high correlation. Combining them provides less diversification benefit than combining across sectors.

Equity + Gold: Gold has near-zero or negative correlation with equities. Adding gold to an equity portfolio meaningfully reduces risk — a classic diversification strategy used by Indian institutional investors.

Expected return — scenario analysis
E(R) = Σ pᵢ × Rᵢ
pᵢProbability of scenario i occurring (must sum to 1)
RᵢReturn in scenario i
Example: Boom (p=0.30, R=+25%), Normal (p=0.50, R=+10%), Recession (p=0.20, R=−15%).
E(R) = 0.30×25 + 0.50×10 + 0.20×(−15) = 7.5 + 5.0 − 3.0 = 9.5%
Variance and standard deviation
σ² = Σ pᵢ × (Rᵢ − E(R))²
σ = √σ²
Continuing example (E(R) = 9.5%):
σ² = 0.30×(25−9.5)² + 0.50×(10−9.5)² + 0.20×(−15−9.5)²
= 0.30×240.25 + 0.50×0.25 + 0.20×600.25
= 72.075 + 0.125 + 120.05 = 192.25
σ = √192.25 = 13.87%
Portfolio expected return (2 assets)
E(Rp) = wA × E(RA) + wB × E(RB)
wA, wBWeights — fraction of portfolio in each asset. Must sum to 1.
Example: 60% Reliance E(R)=14%, 40% ITC E(R)=10%.
E(Rp) = 0.60×14 + 0.40×10 = 8.4 + 4.0 = 12.4%
Portfolio return is simply the weighted average — no interaction effect.
Portfolio variance (2 assets)
σ²p = wA²σ²A + wB²σ²B + 2wAwBCov(A,B)
Cov(A,B)= ρAB × σA × σB
Key insight: When ρ < 1, portfolio σ is LESS than the weighted average of individual σs. This gap is the diversification benefit. When ρ = −1, it is possible to build a zero-variance portfolio by choosing the right weights.

The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) answers a precise question: given a stock's systematic risk, what annual return should investors demand? The answer is the required return on equity (Re) — which flows directly into the WACC formula in Module 5.

Beta — the measure of systematic risk

Beta (β) measures how much a stock moves relative to the overall market.

β = 1.0 → moves exactly with the market (Nifty 50).
β > 1.0 → amplifies market moves — more volatile, more systematic risk.
β < 1.0 → dampens market moves — less volatile, less systematic risk.
β = 0 → no correlation with market (e.g. cash).

Beta only captures systematic risk. A stock with high total volatility but low beta contributes little systematic risk to a diversified portfolio.

The Security Market Line (SML)

The SML is a straight line plotting expected return against beta. Every fairly priced asset lies on the SML. The y-intercept is Rf (return when β=0); the slope is the market risk premium (Rm−Rf).

Stock above SML → undervalued (offers more return than CAPM requires for its beta — investors will buy it, driving the price up until it falls back to the SML).

Stock below SML → overvalued (offers less return than required — investors sell, price falls until it returns to SML).

CAPM connects Module 4 to Module 5

Re from CAPM = cost of equity in WACC. This is the critical link between the two modules. You use CAPM here to find Re. Then in Module 5 you plug that Re into: WACC = (E/V)×Re + (D/V)×Rd×(1−Tc). Without Re, you cannot compute WACC. Without WACC, you cannot do NPV. Module 4 is the engine room of Module 5.
CAPM IN PRACTICE — INDIAN MARKET

Risk-free rate: 10-year Government of India G-Sec yield ≈ 7.1% (2024 benchmark).

Market risk premium: Historical Nifty 50 excess return over G-Secs ≈ 5–6%. Standard exam assumption: 5.5%.

HDFC Bank (β=1.20): Re = 7.1 + 1.20×5.5 = 7.1 + 6.6 = 13.7%. Shareholders demand 13.7% annual return to hold HDFC Bank. This is the bank's cost of equity.

ITC (β=0.60): Re = 7.1 + 0.60×5.5 = 7.1 + 3.3 = 10.4%. Lower beta → lower required return → ITC's equity is cheaper than HDFC Bank's.

CAPM — Security Market Line equation
E(Ri) = Rf + βi × (E(Rm) − Rf)
E(Ri)Required return on asset i = Re (cost of equity)
RfRisk-free rate — 10-yr G-Sec yield in India ≈ 7.1%
βiBeta of asset i — systematic risk measure
E(Rm)−RfMarket risk premium — extra return for bearing market risk ≈ 5.5% (India)
HDFC Bank: E(R) = 7.1 + 1.20×5.5 = 13.7% → This is Re, the cost of equity for WACC.
Beta — definition and estimation
β = Cov(Ri, Rm) / Var(Rm)
Cov(Ri,Rm)Covariance of stock i returns with market (Nifty 50) returns
Var(Rm)Variance of market returns
Estimation: Run a regression of weekly stock returns (y-axis) against Nifty 50 returns (x-axis). The slope coefficient of that regression is beta. Alternatively, use published betas from NSE, Bloomberg or CRISIL.
Portfolio beta
βp = Σ wᵢ × βᵢ
wᵢWeight of stock i in the portfolio
βᵢBeta of stock i
Example: 50% Infosys (β=0.85) + 50% HDFC Bank (β=1.20).
βp = 0.5×0.85 + 0.5×1.20 = 0.425 + 0.60 = 1.025.
Portfolio beta is just the weighted average — unlike portfolio variance, no interaction term needed.
