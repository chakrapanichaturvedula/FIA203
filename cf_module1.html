Corporate finance answers three fundamental questions that every firm — from a Bengaluru startup to a Nifty 50 giant — must answer continuously. All three flow from a single goal: maximising shareholder wealth.
PILLAR 1 · CF-I MODULE 3

Investment decision

Which long-term assets should the firm invest in? This is capital budgeting — evaluating projects using NPV, IRR, and payback period.

Example: Should Tata Motors invest ₹18,000 cr in a new EV plant?

LEFT SIDE OF BALANCE SHEET
PILLAR 2 · CF-I MODULE 5 + CF-II

Financing decision

How should the firm raise the money? Debt vs equity mix, cost of capital, WACC. CF-I covers cost of capital. Capital structure theory and sources of finance are in CF-II.

Example: Should Zomato raise funds via IPO (equity) or bonds (debt)?

RIGHT SIDE OF BALANCE SHEET
PILLAR 3 · CF-II

Working capital decision

How should the firm manage its day-to-day cash flows? Receivables, payables, inventory. Ensures the firm stays liquid and operationally smooth.

Example: How many days of credit should Infosys extend to clients?

CURRENT ASSETS & LIABILITIES

The goal of the firm

Maximise shareholder wealth — not accounting profit, not sales, not market share. Shareholder wealth = the present value of all future cash flows to equity holders, reflected in the current stock price. This goal accounts for both the magnitude and timing of cash flows, and the risk involved.
ObjectiveWhat it ignoresWhy it fails as a goal
Maximise profitTiming of cash flows, riskShort-term profits can be boosted by cutting R&D — destroys long-term value
Maximise salesProfitability, capital costA firm can grow revenues while destroying shareholder value
Maximise shareholder wealthNothing significantIncorporates time, risk, and magnitude — the correct goal

Agency problem and corporate governance

Shareholders (principals) hire managers (agents) to run the firm. But managers may pursue their own interests — empire building, excessive perks, avoiding risk to protect their jobs.

Agency costs

Monitoring costs, bonding costs, residual loss. The cost of misalignment between manager and shareholder goals.

Solutions

Performance-linked pay (ESOPs), independent board, SEBI regulations, threat of hostile takeover.

Indian context

Promoter-owned firms (Reliance, Tata) face different agency dynamics than professionally managed firms. Promoter interests can align or diverge from minority shareholders.

Important distinction: The firm's goal is to maximise shareholder wealth — but it must operate ethically and within legal boundaries. Firms that maximise short-term stock price by breaking laws (like the 2009 Satyam scandal) ultimately destroy wealth.
INDIAN MARKET EXAMPLES

Investment decision: Reliance Jio — ₹1,50,000 cr investment in telecom infrastructure in 2016. Classic capital budgeting: huge upfront cost, long-term cash flow payoff.

Financing decision: Infosys ₹9,200 cr buyback in 2022 — returning capital to shareholders instead of retaining cash. A financing decision that signals confidence in firm value.

Working capital: A seasonal firm like Haldiram's must manage inventory spikes before Diwali without running out of cash — pure working capital management.

Accounting profit vs shareholder wealth
Accounting profit = Revenue − Costs
Shareholder wealth = P₀ = PV of all future cash flows
P₀Current stock price = present value of expected dividends/cash flows
PVPresent value — discounts future CFs at the required rate of return
Key insight: Accounting profit ignores timing and risk. Two firms can have identical profits but very different shareholder values depending on how risky and how far in the future those profits are.
Single-period return
R = (P₁ − P₀ + D₁) / P₀
RTotal return over one period
P₁Price at end of period
P₀Price at start of period
D₁Dividend received during the period
Example: You buy Infosys at ₹1,400. It pays ₹34 dividend and the price rises to ₹1,540. Return = (1540 − 1400 + 34)/1400 = 12.4%
Agency cost relationship
Agency cost = Monitoring + Bonding + Residual loss
MonitoringCost to shareholders of overseeing managers (audits, board meetings)
BondingCost to managers of committing to act in shareholders' interests (contracts, ESOPs)
ResidualRemaining loss even after monitoring and bonding
Example: A CEO's ESOP plan (bonding cost) aligns interests by making the CEO a shareholder — reduces residual loss.

A rupee today is worth more than a rupee tomorrow. This single idea — the time value of money — underpins every formula in corporate finance. It explains why firms discount future cash flows, why bond prices fall when interest rates rise, and why a 30-year SIP creates more wealth than a lump sum invested later.

Why does money have time value?

Opportunity cost

Money received today can be invested immediately and earn a return. Waiting means losing that return.

Inflation

₹100 today buys more goods than ₹100 next year. Purchasing power erodes over time.

Risk

A future payment is uncertain. You might not receive it. The further away, the riskier.

Compounding — money growing forward

If you invest ₹100 at 10% per year, it grows to ₹110 after year 1. In year 2 you earn interest on ₹110 — not just the original ₹100. This is compounding: interest on interest.

NSC / PPF EXAMPLE

₹1,00,000 invested in PPF at 7.1% compounded annually. After 15 years: ₹1,00,000 × (1.071)¹⁵ = ₹2,80,543. The extra ₹80,543 beyond simple interest is the compounding effect.

Discounting — bringing money back to today

Discounting is compounding in reverse. If someone will pay you ₹110 one year from now and your discount rate is 10%, the present value is ₹100. You are "discounting" the future payment to find its equivalent value today.

Key connection: Discounting and compounding are mirror images. FV = PV × (1+r)ⁿ rearranges to PV = FV / (1+r)ⁿ. The same formula, viewed from different directions.

Annuities and perpetuities

Ordinary annuity

Equal payments at the end of each period. EMI on a home loan. Coupon payments on a bond.

Annuity due

Equal payments at the start of each period. Rent paid at the beginning of the month.

Perpetuity

Equal payments forever. British consols. Preference shares with no maturity. PV = C/r.

Growing perpetuity

Payments that grow at rate g forever. Stock valuation (DDM) uses this. PV = C/(r−g).

Growing annuity

Payments that grow at rate g for n periods. Salary growing with inflation over a career. Most complex TVM formula.

INDIAN MARKET EXAMPLES

SIP (Systematic Investment Plan): ₹5,000/month for 20 years at 12% CAGR = ₹49.9 lakhs. This is the future value of an annuity — compounding over time.

Home loan EMI: A ₹50 lakh loan at 8.5% for 20 years → EMI = ₹43,391. This is present value of an annuity — the bank discounts your future payments.

Stock valuation: If Hindustan Unilever pays ₹22 dividend, growing at 5% forever, and your required return is 9%, the stock is worth ₹22/(0.09−0.05) = ₹550. This is a growing perpetuity.

Future value — compounding
FV = PV × (1 + r)ⁿ
FVFuture value — what the investment grows to
PVPresent value — the amount invested today
rInterest / discount rate per period
nNumber of periods
Example: ₹1,00,000 at 8% for 5 years → FV = 1,00,000 × (1.08)⁵ = ₹1,46,933
Present value — discounting
PV = FV / (1 + r)ⁿ
PVPresent value today
FVFuture cash flow to be received
rDiscount rate (= opportunity cost of capital)
nPeriods until the cash flow is received
Example: Receive ₹1,46,933 in 5 years, discount rate 8% → PV = 1,46,933/1.08⁵ = ₹1,00,000
Present value of an ordinary annuity
PV = C × [1 − (1+r)⁻ⁿ] / r
CEqual cash flow per period (coupon, EMI, SIP)
rInterest / discount rate per period
nTotal number of periods
Example: EMI on ₹50L loan at 8.5%/12 monthly for 240 months → PV of payments = ₹50L (bank's present value of receiving your EMIs)
Present value of a perpetuity
PV = C / r
CConstant cash flow received every period forever
rDiscount rate
Example: Preference share paying ₹8 dividend forever, required return 8% → PV = 8/0.08 = ₹100. An infinite stream still has finite value!
Present value of a growing perpetuity
PV = C₁ / (r − g)
C₁Cash flow at end of period 1 (next period)
rDiscount rate (must be > g)
gConstant growth rate of cash flows
Example: HUL dividend ₹22 growing at 5%/year, required return 9% → P = 22/(0.09−0.05) = ₹550. This is the Gordon Growth Model for stock valuation.
Present value of a growing annuity
PV = C₁ × [1 − ((1+g)/(1+r))ⁿ] / (r−g)
C₁First cash flow
rDiscount rate
gGrowth rate per period
nNumber of periods (finite)
Example: Salary starts at ₹6L, grows 5%/year for 30 years, discount rate 10% → PV of career earnings = ₹1.02 cr
