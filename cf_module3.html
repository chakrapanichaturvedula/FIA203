Capital budgeting is the process of evaluating and selecting long-term investments. The firm has limited capital — it must choose which projects to fund. The correct rule: accept all projects with positive NPV, because they increase shareholder wealth by more than their cost of capital.

The four main techniques

Net Present Value (NPV)

PV of all future cash inflows minus the initial investment, discounted at WACC. The theoretically correct method. Accept if NPV > 0.

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

The discount rate that makes NPV = 0. Accept if IRR > WACC (hurdle rate). Easy to understand but has pitfalls with mutually exclusive projects.

Payback period

How many years to recover the initial investment from undiscounted cash flows. Simple, widely used — but ignores time value of money and cash flows after payback.

Discounted payback

Same as payback but uses discounted cash flows. Better than simple payback — but still ignores cash flows beyond the payback point.

Profitability Index (PI)

PI = PV of future CFs / Initial investment. PI > 1 means accept. Useful when capital is rationed — rank projects by PI to maximise value per rupee invested.

The NPV–IRR conflict

When NPV and IRR give conflicting rankings for mutually exclusive projects, always follow NPV. IRR assumes reinvestment at the IRR rate — often unrealistic. NPV assumes reinvestment at WACC — the actual cost of capital. IRR also fails when cash flows change sign multiple times (multiple IRRs problem).

INDIAN MARKET EXAMPLES

Tata Steel — Kalinganagar expansion: ₹23,500 cr project evaluated over 10 years. NPV positive at assumed WACC of 10.5% → approved. IRR was 14.2% — above hurdle. Both methods agreed here.

Infosys — Campus vs Cloud: A new physical campus (high upfront, long payback) vs cloud infrastructure (lower capex, faster payback). Payback favoured cloud; NPV favoured campus. Management chose NPV — the theoretically correct metric.

CFtNet cash flow in period t
rDiscount rate = WACC
nLife of the project in periods

IRR definition
NPV = 0 when r = IRR
IRRThe discount rate that sets NPV to zero. Solved iteratively (trial and error or financial calculator).
Decision rule: Accept if IRR > WACC. If IRR = 14% and WACC = 10% → accept. If IRR = 8% and WACC = 10% → reject. For MUTUALLY EXCLUSIVE projects, always rank by NPV, not IRR.
Payback period
PB = Year before full recovery + (Remaining cost / CF in recovery year)

After Yr2: recovered ₹80K. Remaining = ₹20K. Yr3 CF = ₹30K.
PB = 2 + (20,000/30,000) = 2.67 years

The hardest part of capital budgeting is not the NPV formula — it is identifying the correct cash flows to discount. Only incremental, after-tax cash flows belong in the analysis. Many common-sense items must be excluded.

The golden rules of project cash flows

Incremental only

Only cash flows that CHANGE because of the project. The firm's existing fixed overhead does NOT change → exclude it.

Exclude sunk costs

Money already spent that cannot be recovered. A ₹5 cr feasibility study paid last year is gone whether you proceed or not → irrelevant to the NPV decision.

Include opportunity costs

The value of the next best use of a resource. If the firm uses its own land for a project, the forgone rent is a real cost — include it.

Include side effects (erosion)

If a new product cannibalises existing product sales, the lost revenue is an incremental cost. Maruti's new model reducing sales of an existing model → include this.

After-tax cash flows

Always use after-tax figures. Depreciation is not a cash flow but creates a tax shield — include the tax saving from depreciation.

Net Working Capital (NWC)

Projects often tie up cash in receivables and inventory. NWC increases are outflows at the start; recovered (inflow) at project end.

Three components of operating cash flow

OCF = EBIT + Depreciation − Taxes
Equivalently: OCF = (Sales − Costs) × (1−T) + Depreciation × T
The second term (Dep × T) is the depreciation tax shield — the cash saved on taxes because depreciation reduces taxable income.
MARUTI SUZUKI — NEW MODEL LAUNCH

Initial investment: ₹2,400 cr plant + ₹300 cr NWC = ₹2,700 cr outflow at t=0.

Sunk cost excluded: ₹80 cr R&D spent over the past 2 years → irrelevant, already spent.

Opportunity cost included: The factory site could have been leased for ₹40 cr/year → include as annual cost.

Erosion included: New Alto EV expected to cannibalise 15,000 units/year from existing Alto petrol → include lost contribution margin.

Terminal CF: At year 10, plant sold for ₹400 cr salvage + ₹300 cr NWC recovered = ₹700 cr inflow.

Operating Cash Flow (OCF) — three equivalent forms
OCF = EBIT + Dep − Taxes
OCF = (Sales − Costs)(1−T) + Dep×T
OCF = Net income + Dep (if no interest)
Dep×TDepreciation tax shield — cash saved on tax due to depreciation deduction
TCorporate tax rate
Example: Sales ₹500L, Costs ₹300L, Dep ₹50L, T = 25%.
OCF = (500−300)(0.75) + 50(0.25) = 150 + 12.5 = ₹162.5L
Total project cash flow
CF = OCF − ΔNWC − Capital expenditure
ΔNWCChange in net working capital. Increase in NWC = cash outflow.
CapexCapital expenditure on fixed assets
Year 0: CF = 0 − 300 − 2,400 = −₹2,700 cr (Maruti example)
Operating years: CF = OCF − ΔNWC (usually 0 in mid-years)
Terminal year: CF = OCF + NWC recovered + Salvage value (after-tax)
After-tax salvage value
After-tax salvage = S − T×(S−BV)
SSalvage (sale) price of asset at end of project
BVBook value (accounting value) of asset at that time
T×(S−BV)Tax on the gain if S > BV. Tax saving if S < BV.
Example: Asset sold for ₹400 cr, book value ₹250 cr, T = 25%.
After-tax salvage = 400 − 0.25×(400−250) = 400 − 37.5 = ₹362.5 cr
